## 9.1 A claim with infinitely many cases

Here is a fact nobody doubts: *for every natural number n, it holds
that n + m = m + n.* You have believed it since elementary school, and
you have checked it — what, a few hundred times? A few thousand?

That's the problem. The claim is about *every* number, and there are
infinitely many. No amount of checking cases will ever finish, and
everything we've built so far — the connective rules of Chapter 2, the
quantifier rules of Chapter 4, the equational rewriting of Chapter 5 —
lets us handle only finitely many things at a time. `intro n` gives us
an *arbitrary* number, sure. But then what? We know nothing about `n`
except that it's a number, and "it's a number" was never enough to push
a calculation through.

Except — it is. That's this chapter's idea, and it's one of the
biggest in mathematics. Being a natural number is not a formless
property. Every natural number was *built*, step by step, from zero,
and anything built step by step can be *proved about* step by step.
The proof principle that captures this is called **induction**, and it
is the first place in this book where a statement can be obvious and
its proof can still be genuine work. Let's meet the numbers properly
first.

## 9.2 Numbers are built, not found

Cetacea's natural numbers — the type `Nat` you glimpsed in Chapter 4's
mistakes file, when a stray `0` was offered as a witness where a
`Person` was owed — are generated by exactly two rules:

- `0` is a natural number;
- if `n` is a natural number, so is `succ(n)` — the *successor* of
  `n`, i.e. `n + 1`.

That's the entire species. `1` is shorthand for `succ(0)`, `2` for
`succ(succ(0))`, and the numeral `5` that you type in a Cetacea file
is sugar for a five-story tower of `succ`s. There is no separate fact
of the matter about what `3` is: it *is* `succ(succ(succ(0)))`.

Arithmetic is then defined by recursion on this structure. Addition,
built in, obeys two equations:

```text
add(0, m)       = m
add(succ(n), m) = succ(add(n, m))
```

Read the second one aloud: "(n+1) + m is one more than n + m." Adding
peels `succ`s off the *first* argument, one at a time, until it hits
`0`. So a concrete sum is something the checker can simply *run*:

```text
theorem two_plus_three : add(2, 3) = 5 := by
  refl
```

`refl`, from Chapter 5, normalizes both sides. `add(2, 3)` steps to
`succ(add(1, 3))`, then `succ(succ(add(0, 3)))`, then
`succ(succ(3))` — a five-story tower. The right side, `5`, is the same
tower. Identical terms, `refl` closes, done. A calculation *is* a
proof.

## 9.3 What computation gives you for free

Computation handles more than concrete numerals. The additive
identities hold by pure evaluation, even with a variable in play:

```text
theorem add_zero_left_demo (n : Nat) : add(0, n) = n := by
  refl

theorem add_zero_right_demo (n : Nat) : add(n, 0) = n := by
  refl
```

The first is just the first defining equation. The second is more
interesting: the definition recurses on the *first* argument, and
`n` there is symbolic — yet Cetacea's simplifier also knows the
mirror-image "textbook" equations (`add(n, 0) = n`,
`add(n, succ(m)) = succ(add(n, m))`), so this too computes. Multiplying
by small constants, subtracting zero, comparing with zero — a
surprising amount of everyday arithmetic closes with a bare `refl`.
(Exercise 9.1 asks you to find out whether `mul(1, n) = n` is in this
happy family.)

## 9.4 ...and where computation gets stuck

Now try the chapter's opening claim the same way. The file
[`code/ch09-mistakes.ctea`](code/ch09-mistakes.ctea) — intended to
fail, as usual — makes the attempt:

```text
theorem comm_by_computation (n m : Nat) : add(n, m) = add(m, n) := by
  refl
```

```text
error: docs/book/code/ch09-mistakes.ctea:15: theorem `comm_by_computation` failed: refl cannot prove `add(n, m) = add(m, n)` because the sides are not identical
  note: target: add(n, m) = add(m, n)
  help: Use equality simplification first
    `refl` closes goals whose two sides are already identical. Try `simp` or `rewrite` before `refl` if the sides should compute to the same term.
    try:
      simp
      refl
```

(And no, the suggested `simp` doesn't save this one — try it.) The
equations for `add` need to know whether the first argument is `0` or
a `succ`, and a bare variable `n` is neither: it's a number whose
construction we can't see. The term is **stuck**. `add(n, m)` and
`add(m, n)` are both stuck, they are not the same expression, and
`refl` — quite rightly — refuses to pretend otherwise.

So here is precisely what we're missing: a way to reason about a
number we can't see, *using the fact that it was built from `0` by
`succ`*. That principle has a name.

## 9.5 Induction: the ladder

**The principle of induction.** To prove `P(n)` for every natural
number `n`, it suffices to prove two things:

1. **Base case:** `P(0)`.
2. **Inductive step:** for an arbitrary `k`, *if* `P(k)` holds, then
   `P(succ(k))` holds.

The metaphor everyone uses, because it's perfect: a ladder. The base
case puts your foot on the first rung. The step proves that from any
rung you can always reach the next. Together they get you to every
rung — because every rung, like every number, is finitely many steps
up from the bottom. Notice how exactly the principle mirrors the way
`Nat` is built: one proof obligation for `0`, one for `succ`. That's
no coincidence, and Chapter 11 will make a whole meal of it.

In Cetacea the principle is the tactic `induction n with`, and its two
obligations are two *arms*, written like the arms of `cases`:

```text
induction n with
| zero =>
    ...tactics proving P(0)...
| succ k ih =>
    ...tactics proving P(succ(k))...
```

In the `succ` arm you get two things: `k`, the predecessor — a fresh
name, arbitrary, exactly like an `intro`'d variable — and `ih`, the
**induction hypothesis**: a proof of `P(k)`, the statement one rung
down. Everything difficult and wonderful about induction lives in that
little hypothesis, so let's watch it work.

## 9.6 The first climb

A gentle start: every number minus itself is zero. (Cetacea's `sub` is
truncated subtraction — it bottoms out at zero rather than going
negative — with equations `sub(0, m) = 0`, `sub(n, 0) = n`, and
`sub(succ(n), succ(m)) = sub(n, m)`. Like `add(n, m)`, the term
`sub(n, n)` is stuck while `n` is a variable, so this genuinely needs
the ladder.)

```text
theorem sub_self_demo (n : Nat) : sub(n, n) = 0 := by
  induction n with
  | zero =>
      refl
  | succ k ih =>
      simp
      exact ih
```

Two arms, two little proofs. The `zero` arm owes
`|- sub(0, 0) = 0` — computation, `refl`. The `succ` arm opens at

```text
k : Nat, ih : sub(k, k) = 0  |-  sub(succ(k), succ(k)) = 0
```

`simp` fires the third `sub` equation, peeling one `succ` off each
side of the subtraction:

```text
k : Nat, ih : sub(k, k) = 0  |-  sub(k, k) = 0
```

And now look: the goal *is* the induction hypothesis, verbatim.
`exact ih` closes it. This is the pattern in its purest form — the
step case reduces "the claim at `k+1`" to "the claim at `k`", and the
claim at `k` is exactly what `ih` hands you for free.

## 9.7 Commutativity: the real work

Now the opening claim, for real. This proof is `add_comm` in
`std/nat.ctea`; here it is with a `_demo` name, since the prelude
already owns the real one:

```text
theorem add_comm_demo (n m : Nat) : add(n, m) = add(m, n) := by
  induction n with
  | zero =>
      simp
      refl
  | succ k ih =>
      simp
      rewrite <- ih
      refl
```

The `zero` arm owes `add(0, m) = add(m, 0)`: both sides compute to
`m` (one by the defining equation, one by the textbook mirror), so
`simp` then `refl` closes it.

The `succ` arm is where induction earns its keep. It opens at

```text
k, m : Nat, ih : add(k, m) = add(m, k)  |-  add(succ(k), m) = add(m, succ(k))
```

`simp` computes both sides one step — the left by the defining
equation, the right by the textbook one — giving

```text
ih : add(k, m) = add(m, k)  |-  succ(add(k, m)) = succ(add(m, k))
```

So close! The two sides differ only in that one says `add(k, m)` where
the other says `add(m, k)` — and `ih` says those are *equal*. This is
Chapter 5's move: `rewrite <- ih` finds the right-hand side of `ih` in
the goal — the `add(m, k)` — and rewrites it back to `add(k, m)`,
leaving `succ(add(k, m)) = succ(add(k, m))`. `refl`. The ladder is
climbed: computation does the bookkeeping at each rung, and the
induction hypothesis carries the actual content up from the rung
below.

Pause and appreciate what `ih` is *not*. It is not the theorem — you
don't get to assume what you're proving. It is the statement **at `k`
only**, one rung down, granted while you prove the statement at
`succ(k)`. The circularity is only apparent: by the time the principle
uses your step proof at rung 17, rungs 0 through 16 are already
secured. And when you misuse it, the checker shows you exactly the gap
— which brings us to the mistakes file.

## 9.8 Common mistakes

Run [`code/ch09-mistakes.ctea`](code/ch09-mistakes.ctea). You've
already seen its first error (Section 9.4); here are the other three.

**Mistake 2: treating `ih` as the whole theorem.** The proof below
reaches the `succ` arm and immediately plays `exact ih`:

```text
error: docs/book/code/ch09-mistakes.ctea:26: theorem `ih_is_not_the_theorem` failed: exact proof does not solve the goal: proof has type `add(k, m) = add(m, k)`, but expected `add(succ(k), m) = add(m, succ(k))`
  note: target: add(succ(k), m) = add(m, succ(k))
```

(As in Chapter 2, we trim the generic `help:` paragraph that follows —
you'll see it in your terminal.) Read the two formulas in the message
side by side — the error *is* the
lesson. What you hold is the claim at `k`; what you owe is the claim
at `succ(k)`. They are one rung apart, and the whole job of the step
case is to build that rung (here: `simp` to expose the `succ`s, then
`rewrite <- ih`). If `exact ih` ever closes a step case immediately,
either the statement didn't depend on `n` at all, or something is
wrong.

**Mistake 3: shadowing.** It's tempting to name the predecessor `n` —
after all, it's "the same variable," right?

```text
error: docs/book/code/ch09-mistakes.ctea:31: theorem `shadowed_binder` failed: induction binder `n` would shadow an existing variable
  note: target: sub(n, n) = 0
```

It is not the same variable. The theorem's `n` is the number you're
proving the claim about; the arm's binder is a *fresh* arbitrary
predecessor. Letting one name mean both would be a lie with soundness
consequences, so the checker flatly refuses. Pick a fresh name — `k`
is traditional — and this never bites. (The same freshness rule
applies to `cases` arms and to `intro`, as you may have discovered
already.)

**Mistake 4: inducting underneath a hypothesis about `n`.** This
attempt `intro`s the premise `le(1, n)` and *then* starts induction:

```text
error: docs/book/code/ch09-mistakes.ctea:43: theorem `square_positive` failed: cannot induct on `n` while hypothesis `h` depends on it
  note: target: le(1, mul(n, n))
  help: Revert dependent hypotheses before induction
    Hypothesis `h` mentions `n`, so it cannot be kept unchanged while `n` is split into cases. Use `revert h` to move it back into the goal, run induction, then introduce `h` inside each arm.
    try:
      revert h
      induction n with
      | zero =>
          intro h
          ...
      | succ k ih =>
          intro h
          ...
```

The trouble: if the proof splits `n` into `0` and `succ(k)` cases,
what becomes of `h : le(1, n)`? It talks about the original `n`, and
Cetacea cannot keep that same assumption unchanged across both cases. Rather
than do something subtly wrong, it stops you. Use `revert h` to put the premise
back into the goal, then induct and `intro` it separately inside each arm — i.e., prove
`le(1, n) -> le(1, mul(n, n))` by induction on `n` with the
implication as the goal, not the hypothesis.

## 9.9 Exercises

Open [`code/ch09-exercises.ctea`](code/ch09-exercises.ctea) and clear
the `sorry` flags. For each: first decide whether it's *computation*
(`refl`, maybe with `simp`) or a genuine *climb* (`induction`). Half
the skill is telling them apart.

- **Exercise 9.1** `mul(1, n) = n` — one of these is not like the
  others.
- **Exercise 9.2** `le(n, succ(n))` — every number is at most its
  successor. After `simp`, the `succ` arm is exactly `ih`; the base
  closes with `simp` then `trivial` (recall `le(0, m)` computes to
  `True`).
- **Exercise 9.3** `sub(add(n, m), n) = m` — adding then subtracting
  cancels. Pure `sub_self_demo` energy.
- **Exercise 9.4** `le(n, add(n, m))` — a part is at most the whole.
- **Exercise 9.5** `add(k, add(n, m)) = add(k, add(m, n))` — no
  induction: rewrite with the library's `add_comm`, instantiated as
  `add_comm {n := m; m := n}` so it says exactly `add(m, n) = add(n, m)`.
- **Exercise 9.6 (challenge)** associativity:
  `add(add(n, m), p) = add(n, add(m, p))`. It's `add_assoc` in
  `std/nat.ctea` — write your own by induction on `n` before peeking.
  (Yes, `exact add_assoc` would "work." You'd be cheating yourself out
  of the second-best induction proof in the book.)
